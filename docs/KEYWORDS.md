Dispatch Optimization: In a general setting, it is the process of using automated algorithms and real-time data to match resources like vehicles, personnel, or power generation with tasks or demand efficiently. Here, it becomes particularly relevant when we have a number of energy generators and each of them different costs and constraints.

Load Shedding: It is the intentional reduction of electricity supply to certain areas or customers to prevent the entire power system from collapsing when demand exceeds supply. It is a last-resort measure used by utilities to maintain grid stability during periods of high demand or when there are disruptions in power generation.

Curtailment: It is the reduction of electricity generation from renewable energy sources, such as wind or solar, when the supply exceeds demand or when there are grid constraints.

Peak Shaving: It is the practice of reducing electricity consumption during periods of peak demand to avoid high energy costs and reduce strain on the power grid. This can be achieved through various strategies, such as using energy storage systems, shifting non-essential loads to off-peak times, or implementing demand response programs.

Concentrated Solar Power (CSP): It is a type of solar energy technology that uses mirrors or lenses to concentrate sunlight onto a small area, typically a receiver, to generate heat.

Time-of-Use (ToU) tariff: A typical pricing scheme in households and businesses where the price of electricity varies by time block — typically cheaper at night, more expensive during evening peak hours (e.g., €0.15/kWh at 2 AM, €0.45/kWh at 6 PM). The schedule is known in advance, which is why deterministic optimization can still handle it.

Exogenous features: These are external variables independent of a system or model, used to improve forecasting accuracy. They are "outside" factors—such as weather data, holiday markers, or marketing spend—whose future values must be known or accurately estimated to predict the model's target

Deterministic equivalent formulation: A reformulation of a stochastic optimization problem into a deterministic one by replacing uncertain parameters with their expected values or scenarios, allowing it to be solved using standard optimization techniques.

Backtesting: It is the offline evaluation of an energy scheduling policy by applying it to historical or simulated realized load, PV, and price data to measure how it would actually have performed, without using any future information that would not have been available at the decision time.

Frequency Containment Reserve: It is a type of ancillary service in power systems that helps maintain the balance between supply and demand by providing additional power or reducing consumption when there are frequency deviations in the grid.

Feed-in-tariffs: These are policy mechanisms designed to encourage the adoption of renewable energy sources by providing long-term contracts to renewable energy producers, typically based on the cost of generation of each technology.

#### Non-energy technical terms:
REST API: A set of rules and conventions for building and interacting with web services, allowing different software applications to communicate over the internet using standard HTTP methods.

FAST API: A modern, fast (high-performance) web framework for building APIs with Python 3.6+ based on standard Python type hints.

Canary: A canary deployment is a software release strategy that involves rolling out a new version of an application to a small subset of users or servers before making it available to the entire user base. 

Gate: A gate is a control mechanism used in software development and deployment processes to manage the flow of changes or updates. It acts as a checkpoint or decision point that determines whether a particular change or update should proceed to the next stage of the development or deployment pipeline.
